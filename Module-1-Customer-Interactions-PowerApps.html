2.1 — Post Pay Payments: Cash
Processed through Datascape with cash going into our drawer and an invoice created in RQ.
System: Datascape (carrier-linked) + RQ (point of sale invoice)Datascape connects directly to the carrier. RQ is our point-of-sale system. Both are used for every cash post-pay payment.
Always take cash FIRST, then apply the paymentThis is the cardinal rule. If you apply the payment before you confirm the customer has the money, and they come up short, you're stuck. Cash in hand → then submit.
Payments are not easily reversible; instant payments cannot be reversedOnce submitted, getting it reversed is a manager-level escalation at best. Instant payments are 100% locked. Double-check everything: phone number, amount, account.
System shows balance by phone number only — no name displayedThis is a fraud and accuracy risk. The system gives you a balance for a phone number — it doesn't confirm WHO the account holder is. Be paranoid here.
Customer can pay over their balance to bank credit toward future monthsWhen you type the mobile number into Datascape, the system shows a maximum overpayment amount. If the customer wants to pay ahead before a vacation, cash through Datascape is one of the only ways to do it.

2.4 — Prepaid Payments: Cash
Same Datascape system, different menu — but careful math required. Taxes and fees aren't included in the plan price.
Same Datascape system, different menu (rep will be trained)You'll use the same Datascape login but navigate to the prepaid payment section. Your trainer will walk you through it step by step.
Customer must know their plan cost (look it up on iPad if needed)If the customer doesn't know their plan, pull their account on the iPad. Don't guess.
Plan cost ≠ what customer pays — add 5.5% sales tax + $1 911 admin feeA customer comes in with a $35 plan and exactly $35 in cash. That's not enough. They owe $35 + 5.5% tax + $1 admin fee = ~$37.93. If you apply the payment first and they don't have enough, you're stuck.
Build invoice in RQ first → it calculates the totalWorkflow order matters: build the invoice in RQ → RQ does the math → tell the customer the real total → take the cash → give change → apply payment in Datascape. Never skip steps.
Take exact cash → give change → THEN apply paymentThe 911 admin fee and sales tax % are state-specific. We're listing the values for our state — if working in a different state, verify.

2.6 — Device Buyouts: Through Account (Credit/Debit)
The cleanest buyout method — direct to carrier, no store fees, instantly upgrade-eligible.
Access account via mPOS to confirm buyout is the right moveBefore processing, confirm: Is this the right device? Will this stop any active promo credits? Are there multiple devices on the account?
⚠️ Paying off a device with active promo credits can STOP the creditsIf they bought their phone with a $20/month bill credit, the credit applies AGAINST their device payment. One-time, NON-reversible payment direct to carrierNothing in RQ, nothing in cash drawer. This is a direct customer-to-carrier transaction.
VERIFY the right device if multiple devices on accountIf a customer has 4 lines, you'll see 4 device balances. Confirm with the customer which line/device they're paying off. Get them to point to the right phone before you click.
Once paid off (zero balance), line is immediately upgrade-eligibleThe moment that line shows zero balance, the customer can upgrade right then, trade in for promotions, or even trade in for accessory credit. Don't waste this opportunity.

2.7 — Device Buyouts: Cash In Store
Cash buyouts go through Datascape (separate menu) and RQ. No sales tax — it was paid at financing.
Datascape with a separate menu option (rep will be trained)Same Datascape system, different menu path. Your trainer will walk you through the exact steps.

2.8 — In-Store Credit Card Buyouts
⚠️ Special use case only. High store fees. Requires manager approval. Never standalone.
⚠️ ALWAYS requires approval from your direct superior (rep → AM → manager → owner)Sales rep needs AM or manager approval. AM needs manager approval. Manager needs owner approval. No exceptions.

2.9 — Tip: Post Pay Payment → Sale
Never let a postpaid payment be JUST a payment. These customers come in monthly — that's a relationship.
Always engage about the device — model, condition, ageThis isn't an interrogation — it's conversation. You need to know the device to know when to pitch.
Catch them in the LAST few months of device payments, not afterThe car-sales parallel: in car sales, the ideal time to upsell is when the current car is almost paid off — not after. Once the payment drops off, the customer enjoys the lower bill for a few months and any new payment feels like an INCREASE.
Once a device is paid off and the bill drops, upgrade pitches feel like a "bill increase"Concrete example: customer owes 2 more $20 device payments. Sell them a new phone with a $20 device payment. Their bill stays roughly flat. EASY conversation. Wait two months past payoff and that same phone is 'raising my bill by $20.'
Offer plan analysis — check for missed discounts and promosGoing through their plan with them feels like extra-mile service. It also uncovers: missing autopay discount, missing employer discount, eligibility for a better-tier plan, etc.
Add them to the VIP list in Engage CRM for proactive follow-upWe create a lead, periodically check the account for new promos or plan opportunities, and reach out proactively. The pitch to the customer: 'We're your local store. You're already here every month. Let us be the eyes on your account.'

2.10 — Tip: Prepaid Payment → Sale
Prepaid → postpaid conversion is the gold ticket. Lead with the free flagship phone if the promo is active.
Prepay → postpay carrier promotions are excellent (often a free flagship phone)The carrier often offers an iPhone or Galaxy free for prepay → postpay conversions. That's a $650+ device on the table. Lead with this if the promo is active.

2.11 — Tip: Device Buyout → Sale
A buyout is a golden moment — the line is upgrade-eligible immediately and even broken phones have trade-in value.
Once bought out: line is upgrade-eligible and qualifies for all current promosThe moment that line shows zero balance, the customer can upgrade right then, trade in for promotions, or even trade in for accessory credit.
Even BROKEN phones often qualify for trade-in promotions (sometimes up to $1,000)Turn a bad day into a good day: most buyouts happen because the phone is broken or unwanted. Customer is stressed, paying out hundreds, and feeling like they got a raw deal. You can flip that: 'Look — that phone you just paid off has $500 of trade-in value, even broken. Let's use it.'
Use the trade-in flexibly: accessories, phone buydown, promotionPromos vary, but you can apply trade-in value toward: accessory bundle (free accessories), buying down the new phone (lower upfront cost), bill credits over 36 months (lower monthly), or a cash credit on the invoice. Match it to what the customer values.
Don't let the customer leave with just a lower billIf the buyout closes without a new sale, you've handed the carrier money and gotten nothing for the store. That's a fail.
Replacing the device payment now is easier than adding one back laterSame psychology as the post-pay tip: replace the payment BEFORE the bill drops and the customer gets used to the lower amount.

3.1 — Device Help & Troubleshooting
Our scope is narrow. 5 minutes for a quick fix is fine — beyond that, offer a paid consultation.
Customer expectations are broad; our scope is narrowWhy the scope rule matters: setting up a customer's hearing aid app once means they'll come back for it next month, next year, and forever. We're taking on liability for a product we don't sell.
Stay in scope: Verizon-related issues, phone settings, basic functionalityQuick stuff — adjust a setting, fix a notification, restart a service — keep doing it. Anything beyond 5 minutes that's NOT Verizon-related → consultation appointment.
Out of scope: third-party apps (banking, hearing aids, glucose monitors)These are products we don't sell and can't support. Setting the boundary is not rude — it's professional.
5 minutes for a quick fix is fine — beyond that, offer a paid consultationThe $30 consultation framing: this is NOT charging for normal service. It's charging for our time on out-of-scope work. Frame it as 'going above and beyond' not 'nickel and diming.' Most customers respect it.
$30 / 30-minute consultation (with signed data-loss waiver)Have them sign a data-loss waiver before any deep work. Phones get wiped, accounts get reset — protect the store. Set time expectations up front: 'We have until 11. Let's get as much done as possible.'

3.2 — Billing: Late Payment Cycle / Perceived Double Bill
Customer is paying late every month, accumulating late fees, and seeing stacked balances.
Authenticate in Omni — don't discuss billing without authorization (CPNI)Customer Proprietary Network Information rules — you cannot discuss billing without verifying the person you're speaking with is authorized on the account. Authenticate first, every time.
Show the customer their payment frequency is >30 daysPull up payment history in Omni and walk through it visually. 'April 1 → May 15 = 45 days. Of course you got two bills.' When customers SEE it, they get it.
Explain how late payment + new bill cycle = stacked balanceReframe the problem from 'Verizon is messing up my bill' to 'you're paying too infrequently and we have a fix that saves you money.' That flips the conversation from defensive to constructive.
Solution: enroll them in autopay — $10/smartphone monthly discount + no late feesThe math: $10 autopay discount × 3 phones = $30/month + $5–$7 late fee saved = ~$35–$37/month. That's real money for the customer and a happy interaction for us.
Family of 3 lines = ~$37/month savings (3×$10 autopay + saved late fee)This is a win-win-win: the customer saves money, they stop being frustrated with their bill, and they're now enrolled in autopay which reduces future walk-in payment volume.

3.3 — Billing: Missing or Wrong Promotional Credit
Customer expected a $20/month credit but is seeing $10 or $0. Start with the signed quote.
Pull the signed quote from the original sale day (we keep these)This is exactly why we get signed quotes. Memory is fuzzy, especially when a customer was deciding between multiple phones with multiple promos. The quote is the source of truth.
Compare what they signed for vs. what they're receivingCommon scenario: customer remembers 'the $20 promo' but actually signed for the $15 promo because the $20 had a plan requirement they didn't want. Quote settles it.
If quote matches reality → conversation is done, customer was misrememberingShow them the quote. Walk through it. If everything matches, the conversation is over — politely and professionally.
If quote differs → verify they qualified, nothing has changed, then escalateIf the quote shows they SHOULD be getting more: don't promise anything yet. Verify they qualified at the time, the plan/device hasn't changed (which can disqualify them), and there's no other reason for the discrepancy.
Escalation: bring in a manager; expect resolution within ~7 business days7-day expectation: this isn't a guarantee — it's a reasonable expectation. Most fixes are a phone call to the carrier. Some require the customer to authenticate. Set the expectation, then beat it.

3.4 — Billing: Bill Went Up Without Apparent Change
"My bill went up and I didn't change anything." Sometimes true, sometimes not. Always dig in.
Authenticate in OmniAlways. No exceptions. Authenticate before discussing any billing details.
Look at bills over the last ~12 months — confirm there's an actual changeThe mental anchor problem: customer swears their bill is $120/month. It's been $140 for the last year. They're not lying — they just stopped looking after autopay turned on.
Find the breaking point and dig into line itemsIf the bill was $120 in May and $180 in June, the change happened between those bills. Open the June bill PDF in Omni. Read line items.
Common causes: international charges, store charges, dropped employer discountCommon culprits: international charges (did they travel?), store-billed accessories (a family member shopped at a partner store), employer discount needed re-verification and lapsed, autopay payment failed → autopay discount removed for that month.
If you can't figure it out: call customer service WITH the customerNEVER tell them 'we don't know why, sorry, have a nice day.' That's how customers leave for a competitor. Get a manager involved or call in with the customer present.

3.5 — Billing: Device Buyout Inflating the Bill
A device buyout shows up as a line item on the next bill — but the payment was already made.
Device buyout = line item on next month's billThe buyout has to be accounted for somewhere on the carrier's books. It appears as a line item even though the actual payment is recorded separately.
Payment was already made — they are NOT being double-chargedThe system is balancing — but the customer sees a $500 bill. Walk them through the math so it makes sense.
When you see an unusually high bill, FIRST check for a buyout line itemQuick diagnostic: bill way out of whack from normal? Check for a device payment buyout line item. 9 times out of 10, that's the culprit.
Walk the customer through the math; explain the line itemBring up the bill on Omni. Point to the buyout line item. Point to the payment confirmation. Show them both.
Reset their expectations for next month's normal billOnce you've explained it, set the expectation: 'Next month's bill will be back to your normal amount.' This closes the conversation cleanly.

3.6 — Service Issues: Diagnostic Workflow
Translate vague complaints into actionable diagnoses with structured discovery questions.
Translate the customer's words: incoming, outgoing, audio in, audio out?'Phone's not making calls' can mean: outgoing fails, incoming fails, calls connect but no audio, calls connect but customer can't be heard, etc. Ask which specifically.
Start with basics: silent mode, focus, DND, ringer volumeBefore any diagnostics, rule out the obvious. Customer might have Do Not Disturb on without realizing it.
Outbound test call: dial **#832** (Verizon test number), put on SPEAKERPHONEWhen the customer hears the Verizon automated response themselves, that's a mental check mark. They know outbound calls work. Without that, they'll keep doubting.
Inbound test call: call the phone from store landline, let it ringDon't just see the call pop up and cancel. Let it ring. Show them: 'That's my store number calling you. Inbound works.' Visible confirmation.
Discovery questions: Where? When? With whom? Who else has the issue?If it's only at one location, you can't be there. If everyone in the house on Verizon has the issue → likely network or location. Just one phone → likely device.
Ask customer to screenshot home screen during the issue (SOS, Wi-Fi state, signal)SOS mode? No bars? Not actually on Wi-Fi? Visual evidence helps diagnose what you can't reproduce in the store.

3.7 — Service Issues: Network-Side Fixes
When you genuinely believe there's a service issue, two low-impact tools resolve a lot of cases.
Network settings reset (iPhone & Android, via Settings)On both platforms it's in Settings. Reps will be trained on the exact path. The 'downside' of having to re-enter Wi-Fi passwords is actually useful — it forces re-authentication to the home network, which can fix Wi-Fi-related issues.
Forces network reconnect; clears Wi-Fi credentials (will need to be re-entered)Warn the customer up front that they'll need their Wi-Fi password. Better to set expectations than have them upset when they get home.
SIM regeneration: new eSIM or physical SIM cardSIMs DO go bad; sometimes a swap fixes it. eSIM regeneration or physical SIM swap are both valid fixes.
⚠️ SIM swap fraud is real — verify ID, authentication, phone present, never push to absent phoneAlways verify ID, authenticate, ensure the phone is physically present. Never ever push a SIM to a phone that isn't with you. This is the scenario where a bad actor pretends to be a customer to steal their number.
Beyond these: tech support escalation + manager involvementIf basic fixes don't work, get tech support on the phone WITH the customer, and bring a manager into the loop. Also: verify the phone is on current iOS/Android before chasing service issues — sometimes the fix is 'install the update.'

3.8 — Honest Conversations & Software Frustration
Sometimes customers come in upset that an iOS or Android update changed their phone. Be patient. Be honest.
Updates change things; that's the manufacturer's call, not oursCustomers don't like change. When their phone updates and the icons move or the camera app behaves differently, we get the brunt of it. Accept it.
Benefits (security, features, fixes) usually outweigh customer frustrationThe manufacturer ideology point: buying a phone is buying into that manufacturer's update philosophy. Apple and Samsung will keep updating. Customers can choose to update or not, but they can't keep their phone exactly as it was forever.
Be patient; show them the new featuresWalk through what changed with them. Often what feels like a broken feature is just moved. Show them where it went.
Sometimes the only options are: live with it, factory reset, or new device'We've taken this as far as we can.' 'Your options are X, Y, or Z.' Don't oversell solutions. Customers respect honesty more than overpromising.
Don't make false promises — be honest, bring in backup (manager, seasoned rep)When tough conversations happen, have a manager or seasoned rep present. That voice of authority backs you up and makes the customer feel heard.

Timing matters. Plant the upgrade seed early, catch device payments before payoff, and replace device payments the moment a buyout closes.
Honesty builds trust. Be straight with customers about scope, options, and limitations — then bring in backup when tough conversations arise.
